# Oracle Sites and Healing Sanctuaries
This [map](https://hasanguvendik.github.io/oracle-sites-healing-sanctuaries/) was made for my BA thesis in order to visualise oracular sites and healing sanctuaries of the ancient Mediterranean and its periphery.
## Data
<p align="justify">The dataset was created using multiple sources;

Site names and types was derived from Trevor Curnow's The Oracles of the Ancient World: A Complete Guide (2004). However, inaccuracy of the degree/minutes coordinate system he used (sites were off approx. 4 km) necessitated geocoding. That task was completed using [Recogito](https://recogito.pelagios.org/). Site types and associated gazetteer links were also added at this stage.

To further enrich the data, [ToposText](https://topostext.org/) dataset was used to provide description and links to other sites such as wikis and the ToposText itself. Lastly, Wikimedia images were included in the popup menu to get pictures of the sites but it is possible some pics are missing, irrelevant or so. The reason for this might be the urls queried from wikidata may have brought the very first images.
</p>

## Some Things To Consider

<p align="justify">This data is not comprehensive and there are some problems. First of these problems is Curnow's selection criteria being quite loose. That is determining whether a site is oracular or not, his sources ranges widely from ancient authors such as Strabo, Pausanias, Herodotus to archaeological research done in these sites.  These sources come with inherent problems such as: A statement from an ancient source might simply be wrong or insufficient, some findings might be suggestive rather than indicative. Nevertheless, factors such as these sometimes did not stop Curnow from including a site. To give an example; a sanctuary of Asclepius in Asine is mentioned on p.50, describing its location in Argolis and how it has achieved fame thanks to a well-known poem (The King of Asine by Seferis). It is also here he adds that no trace of Asclepeion had been found in Asine and Pausanias actually makes no mention of it. Leaving the reader wondering the source of information regarding a sanctuary of Asclepius at Asine. This, a major frustration for the researcher is actually a deliberate choice. Prologue to his book, he says " it was decided at an early stage...it would not be possible to include a full scholarly apparatus in the book."  This choice leaves the academically-oriented reader (who is not a tourist of 20 years prior) frustrated but not helpless. 
At first, I have tried to exclude really speculative or ambiguous ones, sites that we are not of their location or do not exist by not tagging them. However, upon realising the herculean job that is I decided to make them visible on the map where possible. These sites are labelled as "Other"

Long story short, the difficulty with Curnow's work is caused by the nature of his book. His choice of not citing in-text (in order to make his work reach a wider audience) has caused some researchers (see Stoneman,p. 65) to question and challenge some of the things in Curnow's work. Despite these issues, Curnow's work is still valuable for giving a glimpse into the pervasiveness of divination. 
By linking sites to major projects such as ToposText and [Pleiades](https://pleiades.stoa.org/) (from there one can also find links to other databases such as Perseus, EDH,PHI,MANTO and many others) , this project hopes the mitigate the aforementioned problems.</p>
